THE HISTORY OF COFFEE IN VIETNAM

The history of coffee in Vietnam really starts with the French and their passion for ‘café’ and it is intrinsically linked to the spread of coffee across the world. Coffee was introduced to Vietnam by the French Colonists in the mid 1800s. The French, like their European counterparts, developed a taste for coffee in the 1600s.

Volataire and Diderot at Cafe Procope
Rousseau discusses the smell of roasting coffee
Coffee Arrives in Vietnam

French involvement in Vietnam began with trade as early as the 17th century, and increased after the loss of French colonies in the Caribbean, culminating in the full colonization of French Indochina by 1887. Coffee was first brought to Vietnam from Bourbon (La Reunion) in the mid 1800s but production remained small. This is likely a result of military conflicts – the French being more concerned with land acquisition than agriculture – although there is little research to explicitly confirm this theory. Coffee production in Vietnam would eventually be disrupted in the 1960s and 70s by the Vietnam War.

Bombs Fall in Vietnam – Raymond Depardon (1972)
Coffee Makes a Comeback

Like most agriculture, the coffee industry was nationalised after the war. The Vietnamese government collectivised the land in the Central Highlands and resettled ethnic groups from the South in order to work on the collective farms. By 1985, state plantations accounted for 70% of the arable land in the provinces of Dak Lak, Gia Lai and Kon Tum, however the Doi Moi reforms of 1986 shifted the focus to a market-driven economy and reinstated the right to privately owned enterprise. This is when coffee production in Vietnam really started to take off. Between 1995 and 1999, coffee production in Vietnam nearly tripled, and has continued to increase since then. Many people are surprised to discover that Vietnam is now the second-largest coffee producer, after Brazil, but that’s a story for another blog…
